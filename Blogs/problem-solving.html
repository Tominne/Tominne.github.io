Time I was blocked by a simple problem:


1: Sometimes things that seem stupid trip you up like, opening a file not in Abuntu and then being unable to perform basic commands, or having console in the wrong repository and wondering why tests are not working, or missing a capital letter and not being able to debug code etc etc.

2: It is tempting to hide when problems feel dumb or you feel like you are too far behind and it would be embarrassing. Some of the time I fiddling by myself for too long and worked it out, sometimes bing chat solved my dilemma for me (pointing out my > greater than symbol is facing the wrong way), and sometimes I asked for help and often was met with fairly lighthearted chill responses which improve my sense of connection as-well as helping me.

3: I felt tired and lacking in motivation some of the time. Sometimes I can fiddle for ages and not get bored. I feel embarrassed getting stuck on basic things or stuff I should have done already, but usually feel okay after asking. I also feel scared to have to problem solve with a tutor as soon as I ask a question, I want to be able to ask stuff and try to remedy it later when i have energy. My asking and coding energy sometimes happens at differing times, even though I know that isn't the most easy way to fix all my code for either of us, but committing my-self to a potentially long back and forth every-time I have a question can on occasion stop me from asking immediately.

4: I learn slowly that there is not as much to be scared of and everyone is human including the tutors especially here at Dev Ac.
I could be cheesy and say yay just ask, there is nothing to be scared of... but anxiety is not rational and there is a warm up period that often just needs to occur before trust is set, and the program should take that into account for some students if it is to be more inclusive and 'fair'.


Time I solved a problem elegantly:


1: A problem was being unable to remove extra spaces between words after remove words I did not want from a sentence.

2: A technique I used was labelling the problem clearly, trying a solution before asking for guidance, then following that guidance down a path of things I had not tried to come to a fix.

3: Throughout the process I felt frustrated at first doing it alone, then amused by peers relating and joking about the issue ands trying to help, then on more of a roll as I found new things to try.

4: I learnt that trying alone doing the same thing again and again is a waste of time and energy even though it can feel easiest to stay quiet and fiddle without reaching elsewhere be it peers or research (yay executive function).

A time I was reluctant to ask for help:


What made me reluctant?
Well, internalized expectation and embarrassment that obviously isn't helpful yet I think would be pretty silly to expect not to have been internalized deeply into many of us by the the narrow minded ableist society we live in.

This goes especially for a girl in a male dominant world LET ALONE in this male dominated topic of learning.
Expecting that anxiety to switch off because 'someone said so' is entirely wishful thinking, but wouldn't that be great.


I have encountered this a few times currently with getting confused about where to locate my console.log output, and I am working on the anxiety with a certain amount of 'exposure therapy' you might call it, by asking, and writing it here, yet there is a warm up time where a space must be supportive and safe that can not be skipped from the other end either.
